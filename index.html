Making Sense of Trigonometry

Why are Cos, Sin, Tan, Sec, Cot and Scs the way they are?

1. Points, Coordinates, and the Distance from the Origin

Think of a 2D plane centered at (0,0). Any point (x, y) on this plane can be infinitely far in any direction—negative or positive. The plane is huge, but one powerful concept shrinks it all down: the distance from the origin (a.k.a. the vector length).

If you pick a point (x, y), the distance r from the origin (0,0) is r = √(x² + y²). This r is like the “hypotenuse” of a right triangle whose sides are the x (horizontal distance) and y (vertical distance).

We can imagine all points that lie exactly 1 unit away from the origin forming the unit circle. Hence (x,y) would satisfy x² + y² = 1.

2. Ratios Instead of Absolute Distances

Because the plane is infinite, measuring absolute distances can get unwieldy. Instead, we focus on ratios that stay the same when we scale a shape up or down. For angles, these ratios become:

  • cos(a): A ratio representing how far horizontally the point is from the center, relative to the overall distance r.
  • sin(a): A ratio representing how far vertically the point is, again relative to r.
If your point (x, y) is on the unit circle (where r = 1), then:

x = cos(a), y = sin(a).

In other words, for an angle a measured from the positive x-axis, (cos(a), sin(a)) are the coordinates on that circle.

3. The Famous Identity sin²(a) + cos²(a) = 1

Why does sin²(a) + cos²(a) = 1 hold?

On the unit circle, every point satisfies x² + y² = 1.

Because x = cos(a) and y = sin(a), we get cos²(a) + sin²(a) = 1.

This isn’t just an arbitrary rule—it’s a basic fact about how circles and coordinates work. You’ve basically taken the Pythagorean theorem and applied it to coordinates (cos(a), sin(a)) on a circle of radius 1.

4. Introducing tan, sec, cot, csc

We can also define other helpful ratios to avoid juggling x, y, and r all the time:

tan(a) = sin(a) / cos(a)
sec(a) = 1 / cos(a)
cot(a) = 1 / tan(a) = cos(a) / sin(a)
csc(a) = 1 / sin(a)

These might look like new functions, but really, they’re just shorthand for different ways of combining sin(a) and cos(a). They help in certain equations where you’ve already calculated a tan(a) and need another ratio quickly—without going back and forth between sin and cos.

5. Dividing by cos²(a) to Get More Identities

If you take the fundamental identity sin²(a) + cos²(a) = 1 and divide every term by cos²(a), you arrive at tan²(a) + 1 = sec²(a).

Step 1: Divide each term by cos²(a):

sin²(a)/cos²(a) + cos²(a)/cos²(a) = 1/cos²(a)

Step 2: From our definitions tan(a) = sin(a)/cos(a) and sec(a) = 1/cos(a), it follows that

  • sin²(a)/cos²(a) = tan²(a)
  • 1/cos²(a) = sec²(a)
  • cos²(a)/cos²(a) = 1

Step 3: Substitute these into the equation and simplify:

tan²(a) + 1 = sec²(a)

Step 4: Make sec(a) the LHS

Since tan²(a) + 1 = sec²(a), take the square root of both sides (noting any sign considerations depending on the quadrant of a):

sec(a) = ±√(tan²(a) + 1)

Typically, if cos(a) is positive (e.g., in the first or fourth quadrant), we choose the positive root. Either way, now you have an expression for sec(a) in terms of tan(a).

Why do this? In some problems, it’s easier to work with tan(a) and sec(a) than to track sin(a) and cos(a) individually. By rewriting the identity in these terms, we get a direct formula linking tan and sec without needing sin or cos explicitly.

Similarly, dividing by sin²(a) yields 1 + cot²(a) = csc²(a). It’s just a rearrangement to let us use cotangent and cosecant instead of sine and cosine.

6. Putting the intuition together

  1. Points and Angles: We start with a point (x, y) on a plane at some distance r from the origin.
  2. Ratios: Define cos(a) = x / r and sin(a) = y / r so we can keep track of horizontal and vertical proportions.
  3. The Unit Circle: If r = 1, then x² + y² = 1 leads straight to sin²(a) + cos²(a) = 1.
  4. Extra Tools: tan, sec, cot, csc are simply convenient ratios or reciprocals to help solve more complex equations without juggling all sides of the triangle (or all coordinates).
  5. Derived Identities: We can divide the core identity by sin²(a) or cos²(a) to rewrite the equation in terms of tan and sec, or cot and csc.

7. Example: Finding sec(a) from tan(a)

Suppose you already know that tan(a) = 2. You might want to figure out sec(a) without going back to sin(a) and cos(a):

Start with tan²(a) + 1 = sec²(a).

Plug in tan(a) = 2, so tan²(a) = 4.

Then 4 + 1 = 5 = sec²(a).

Thus sec(a) = √5.

All you needed was tan²(a) + 1 = sec²(a) to jump straight to sec(a) = √5.

8. Final Thoughts

Trigonometry is not just a set of triangle formulas to memorize—it’s a geometry of rotating points on a plane. sin and cos measure the x and y coordinates of a point on the unit circle.

sin²(a) + cos²(a) = 1 is simply the statement that (cos(a), sin(a)) lies on that circle.

tan, sec, cot, and csc are extra “tools” that come from mixing and matching sin and cos. They become crucial in more advanced math and real-world applications where different side ratios are more convenient.
